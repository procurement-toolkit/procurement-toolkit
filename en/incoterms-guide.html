Quick Notes for Buyers

EXW gives the seller the least responsibility and the buyer the most — you handle everything from the seller's door onward, including export clearance, which can be a real burden if you don't have a local agent.

FOB is the most commonly used term for ocean freight in practice, even though Incoterms 2020 technically recommends FCA for containerized cargo (FOB was originally designed for break-bulk/loose cargo loaded directly onto a vessel, not for cargo handed to a container terminal).

CIF and CFR are sea/inland-waterway-only terms — for containerized shipments where the carrier takes custody at a terminal rather than alongside the ship, CIP and CPT are the more precise modern equivalents.

DDP gives the buyer the least responsibility — the seller handles import clearance and duty in your country, which can be a compliance issue in some jurisdictions since a foreign seller may not be authorized to act as importer of record.

Why Incoterms Matter

Incoterms don't determine who owns the goods or the price — they define exactly where cost and risk transfer from seller to buyer, and who is responsible for each step in between. Getting the wrong term into a purchase order can mean paying for freight or insurance you thought was included, or being uninsured during transit without realizing it.

How to use this guide: Before finalizing a purchase order, confirm which Incoterm the quote is based on, and cross-check who is actually responsible for freight, insurance, and import duty using the table above. Frequently Asked Questions
FOB and FCA both transfer risk at 'point of loading' — what's actually different?

FOB transfers risk when goods are loaded on board the vessel. FCA transfers risk when goods are handed to the carrier at a named place (a terminal or warehouse), which for containerized cargo is usually well before it's actually loaded on board. Because container cargo is physically handed over at a terminal, Incoterms 2020 recommends FCA over FOB for containers.

What's the practical problem with using FOB for containerized cargo?

FOB was originally designed for break-bulk cargo loaded directly onto a vessel. For container shipments, there's a gap between when the container is handed to the terminal and when it's actually loaded on board, which can leave the exact point of risk transfer ambiguous.

    Why Run a Should Cost Analysis?

    Accepting a supplier's quoted price at face value means you have no way of knowing how much margin is built in. A Should Cost Analysis builds up an independent estimate of what a product ought to cost, giving you real leverage in negotiations rather than just asking for "a better price."

    How to use it in practice: Use it to benchmark competing supplier quotes on new parts, or to build a fact-based case for cost reduction with an existing supplier. If labor cost is a large share of the total, ask whether production could shift to a lower-cost region. If machine utilization is assumed low, ask whether committing to higher volume could justify a lower unit price.

    Frequently Asked Questions
    What is Should Cost analysis, and how is it different from just reviewing a quote?

    Should Cost analysis builds an independent cost estimate — materials, processing, overhead, margin — instead of taking a supplier's quote at face value. It gives you a concrete, defensible reference point for negotiation rather than a gut-feel judgment.

    How do I negotiate when a supplier's quote is significantly above my Should Cost estimate?

    Point to the specific cost category driving the gap (materials, labor, overhead, or margin) and ask the supplier to walk through their assumptions for that line. This tends to get a more concrete response than a general request to 'lower the price.'

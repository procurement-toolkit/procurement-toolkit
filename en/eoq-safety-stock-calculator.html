Why an Ordering Rule Matters

Ordering by gut feel usually means tying up cash in excess inventory, or running out and halting production. EOQ, safety stock, and reorder point turn that judgment call into a defensible, repeatable rule.

How to use it in practice: Use it when setting up ordering rules for a new SKU, or when re-checking safety stock for a seasonal item with volatile demand. Revisit the numbers whenever lead time or demand variability changes meaningfully.

Frequently Asked Questions
Does EOQ always mean ordering less?

No — EOQ finds the order quantity that minimizes the combined cost of ordering and holding inventory. Ordering too frequently increases ordering costs; ordering too rarely increases holding costs and stockout risk. EOQ is about minimizing total cost, not simply minimizing order size.

What factors should determine safety stock levels?

Demand variability, lead time uncertainty, and your target service level (acceptable stockout probability) are the key inputs. Longer, less predictable overseas lead times generally call for higher safety stock to avoid running out.

How is Reorder Point different from safety stock?

Reorder Point is the inventory level that triggers a new order; safety stock is the buffer built into that calculation. Reorder Point = (average daily demand x lead time) + safety stock.

The 3 Policy Zones

Invest / Grow: Attractive market, strong relative position. Commit resources, build the relationship, consider joint development.

Selective / Hold: Mixed signals \u2014 either the market is average or your position is average. Invest selectively, maintain the relationship, watch for shifts.

Harvest / Divest: Unattractive market and/or weak position. Minimize new commitment, simplify, or plan an exit.

🔄 Use this alongside your other supplier tools. The Kraljic Matrix is the simpler 2x2 version of this same idea. Check the Supplier Preferencing Matrix for how suppliers view you back, or the Supplier Risk Matrix for continuity risk.
Why Use a Directional Policy Matrix?

The Kraljic Matrix is a solid starting point, but its 2x2 grid forces every category into one of only four boxes, which can feel too coarse for a large or complex portfolio. The Directional Policy Matrix (a nine-box grid popularized by Shell and later refined by GE and McKinsey) uses the same underlying logic with finer granularity, letting you distinguish a category that's borderline-attractive from one that's clearly so, and plan resource allocation with more nuance.

How to use it in practice: Score each supplier or category on both axes using multiple weighted factors rather than a single guess, plot it, and use the resulting zone (Invest, Selective, or Harvest) to set your negotiation posture and time investment. Frequently Asked Questions
How is this different from the Kraljic Matrix?

The Kraljic Matrix uses a simpler 2x2 grid (4 zones) based on supply risk and profit impact. The Directional Policy Matrix uses a finer 3x3 grid (9 cells, collapsed into 3 policy zones) based on supply market attractiveness and your relative strength, giving more nuanced positioning for a large portfolio.

What factors go into "Supply Market Attractiveness"?

Common factors include the number of qualified suppliers, the market's growth rate, the pace of technological change, barriers to entry for new suppliers, and the profitability of suppliers in that market. Score these as a weighted composite rather than a single guess.

What if an item lands exactly on a cell boundary?

Treat scores near a boundary as a signal to look more closely rather than a precise verdict — the matrix is a planning aid, not an exact formula. When in doubt, favor the more conservative (lower) zone for resource commitment decisions.

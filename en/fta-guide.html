Self-Certification vs Authority-Issued Certification

Self-Certification

  • The exporter, producer, or (in some agreements) the importer prepares and signs the Certificate of Origin directly — no third-party stamp required
  • Used in agreements such as USMCA, CPTPP, and most EU trade agreements
  • Faster to issue, but the certifier carries the burden of proof if customs later audits the origin claim
  • Careful review of the origin criteria before signing is essential — an incorrect claim can mean back duties and penalties

Authority-Issued Certification

  • Issued by a government body or an authorized chamber of commerce after reviewing supporting documents
  • Used in agreements such as the ASEAN Trade in Goods Agreement (Form D) and many bilateral agreements across Asia
  • More readily accepted by the importing country's customs authority, but takes longer to obtain
  • Requires supporting documentation (bill of materials, cost breakdown) to be submitted to the issuing authority

Pre-Issuance Checklist
  • Have you confirmed the correct HS code for the exported product?
  • Does the product meet the agreement's rules of origin (wholly obtained, tariff shift, or value-added/regional value content threshold)?
  • Have you prepared supporting documentation (bill of materials, cost breakdown) in case of an origin audit?
  • If self-certifying, have you confirmed whether "approved exporter" or "Registered Exporter (REX)" status is required?
  • Have you compared the preferential tariff rate against the standard (MFN) rate to confirm it's actually worth claiming?
  • Will the Certificate of Origin reach the importer within its validity period (commonly around 12 months, but this varies by agreement)?

Frequently Asked Questions
What's the practical difference between self-certification and authority-issued Certificates of Origin?

Self-certification lets the exporter prepare and sign the C/O directly, which is faster but puts the burden of proof on the exporter if customs later audits the claim. Authority-issued certification involves a government body or chamber of commerce reviewing documents before issuing the C/O — slower, but generally more readily accepted by the importing customs authority.

How long is a Certificate of Origin typically valid?

It varies by agreement, but 12 months from the issue date is common. Always confirm the specific validity period for the agreement you're using, and plan issuance timing so the C/O reaches the importer while still valid.

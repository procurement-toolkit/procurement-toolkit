Why Purchase Price Isn't the Whole Story

Comparing equipment purely on sticker price can lead you to buy something with cheap upfront cost but expensive maintenance or poor energy efficiency. Total Cost of Ownership adds up every cost from acquisition to disposal so you see the true, full-lifecycle cost of each option.

How to use it in practice: Use it when writing a capital expenditure justification, comparing two vendor quotes, or deciding between a cheaper machine with higher running costs and a pricier one that is more efficient long-term.

Frequently Asked Questions
Why shouldn't I just compare purchase price when choosing equipment?

Purchase price is only one part of the total cost. Installation, maintenance, energy consumption, downtime, and disposal costs can add up to make a cheaper-to-buy machine more expensive over its lifetime than a pricier, more efficient one.

What TCO factors get overlooked most often when comparing two quotes?

Energy efficiency (power consumption) and downtime cost (lost production from breakdowns) are the most commonly skipped factors — often because downtime is harder to quantify. In practice, downtime is frequently the single biggest driver of TCO difference between options.

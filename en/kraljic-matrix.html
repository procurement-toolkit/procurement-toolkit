The 4 Quadrants, Explained

Strategic: High spend impact and high supply risk. Requires deep, long-term partnerships and joint planning.

Leverage: High spend impact but low supply risk — many suppliers available. Use competitive bidding to drive price down.

Bottleneck: Low spend but high supply risk. Focus on securing continuity of supply, not price.

Non-Critical: Low spend, low risk, widely available. Simplify and automate the buying process to reduce administrative cost.

Why Use a Kraljic Matrix?

Treating every supplier or category the same way wastes negotiation effort on low-value purchases and under-manages the few relationships that actually carry risk. The Kraljic Matrix (developed by Peter Kraljic, published in Harvard Business Review, 1983) gives procurement teams a simple, defensible way to prioritize where to spend their time.

How to use it in practice: Score each major category or supplier on the two axes, drag it into position, and use the quadrant to decide your approach — aggressive price negotiation for Leverage items, supply continuity planning for Bottleneck items, and executive-level relationship management for Strategic items. Revisit the matrix periodically, since a category's position can shift as markets and your own volume change. Your data is saved automatically in this browser only — nothing is sent to a server.

Frequently Asked Questions
What are the four quadrants of the Kraljic Matrix, and what does each mean?

The matrix plots supply risk against profit impact. Non-critical items (low risk, low impact) call for efficient process automation. Leverage items (low risk, high impact) call for competitive bidding and negotiation leverage. Bottleneck items (high risk, low impact) call for securing supply continuity. Strategic items (high risk, high impact) call for close partnership and joint planning with the supplier.

How often should a Kraljic classification be re-evaluated?

Annually at minimum, and sooner if there's a major shift — a new supplier entering the market, a shortage affecting supply risk, or a significant change in a category's spend. A category's classification isn't permanent and can move between quadrants over time.

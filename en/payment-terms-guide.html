Payment Terms Guide (T/T, L/C, D/P, D/A)

Every payment term shifts risk toward either the seller (exporter) or the buyer (importer). See at a glance which side each term favors.

Term-by-Term Breakdown
Favors Buyer

T/T (Telegraphic Transfer)

  • Simple bank-to-bank wire transfer
  • Can be combined flexibly — advance, on delivery, or installments
  • Simple process, low fees
  • Can be risky with a new counterparty with no trust history
Balanced — Bank Guarantees Payment

L/C (Letter of Credit)

  • The buyer's bank guarantees payment
  • Bank commits to pay once documents (B/L, invoice, etc.) match
  • Common safeguard for large transactions with new counterparties
  • Issuance fees apply, and strict documentary requirements add work
Favors Seller

D/P (Documents against Payment)

  • Buyer must pay before receiving shipping documents
  • Bank handles the documents but does not guarantee payment
  • If the buyer doesn't pay, they cannot claim the goods
Risk to Seller

D/A (Documents against Acceptance)

  • Buyer only needs to accept a bill of exchange (a promise to pay later) to get documents
  • Buyer receives the goods before payment is due
  • Recommended only with a buyer whose creditworthiness is verified
Checklist Before Choosing Payment Terms
  • Do you have a trading history with this counterparty, or is it a new relationship?
  • Is the transaction large enough to warrant a bank payment guarantee (L/C)?
  • What is the foreign exchange or political risk in the counterparty's country?
  • If using L/C, does the buyer have sufficient credit line headroom to open it?
  • Do you know the process for handling a documentary discrepancy?
  • Does the cash recovery timing under these terms fit your cash flow plan?
This guide is a general reference to help you understand the concepts. For actual contract terms or interpreting specific L/C clauses, consult your bank's trade finance team or a qualified professional.

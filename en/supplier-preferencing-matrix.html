The 4 Quadrants, Explained

Core: You represent significant value and the supplier finds you attractive. You likely get their best service and priority access.

Development: Your current spend is modest, but the supplier sees strong growth potential in you. They may invest in the relationship hoping to grow it.

Exploitable: You spend a lot, but the supplier doesn't find the account particularly attractive beyond the revenue. Watch for being taken for granted.

Nuisance: Low value and low attractiveness to the supplier. Expect slower response times and less flexibility.

Why Use a Supplier Preferencing Matrix?

The Kraljic Matrix tells you how you should view a supplier. It doesn't tell you how that supplier views you — and that gap matters. A supplier who sees your account as a "Nuisance" will deprioritize you on service and innovation no matter how strategic they are to your business. The Supplier Preferencing Matrix (developed by Paul Steele and Brian Court, mid-1990s) fills that gap by mapping the relationship from the supplier's side.

How to use it in practice: For each key supplier, estimate the relative value of your account to them and how attractive you are as a customer beyond the money. Plot both this matrix and the Kraljic Matrix for the same supplier — a mismatch (e.g., you see them as Strategic, but they see you as Nuisance) is a warning sign that you may not get the partnership-level treatment you're expecting. Frequently Asked Questions
What's the difference between the Kraljic Matrix and the Supplier Preferencing Matrix?

The Kraljic Matrix maps how a buyer should view a supplier, based on supply risk and profit impact. The Supplier Preferencing Matrix flips the view: it maps how the supplier likely views the buyer, based on account value and attractiveness. Using both together gives a fuller picture of the relationship's power dynamic.

How do I estimate "Account Attractiveness" without asking the supplier directly?

Look for indirect signals: how quickly they respond to your requests, whether they offer you preferential terms or early access to new products, whether they proactively suggest ways to grow the relationship, and how consistent and easy your payment and ordering process has been from their side.

What should I do if I'm in the Exploitable quadrant?

This usually means you give the supplier significant revenue but they don't see much beyond the transaction. Consider consolidating more spend with them to increase leverage, diversifying to reduce dependency, or working to make the account more attractive (longer contracts, more predictable ordering, becoming a reference customer).

Global Raw Material & Economic Indicator Hub

A curated set of indicators procurement and logistics professionals should keep an eye on, linked directly to each official source. Check the linked sites for real-time figures.

PMI (Purchasing Managers' Index)

A leading economic indicator based on surveys of purchasing managers in manufacturing and services. Above 50 signals expansion; below 50 signals contraction.
Why it matters: A turn in major economies' PMI often precedes a shift in demand and order volume a few months later, making it a useful leading signal for inventory and order planning.

LME Base Metal Prices

International benchmark prices for copper, aluminum, zinc, and other base metals traded on the London Metal Exchange (LME).
Why it matters: These directly affect the cost of wiring, piping, electronic components, and other metal-based inputs — worth checking before a quote negotiation.
LME Metal Prices →

Global Steel Price Trends

Reference pricing for hot-rolled coil, cold-rolled coil, rebar, and other steel products across major markets.
Why it matters: Useful supporting data when negotiating cost or pricing adjustments on steel-intensive products.

BDI (Baltic Dry Index)

A benchmark index for international dry bulk shipping rates, reflecting freight cost trends for raw materials and grain.
Why it matters: Spotting a rising freight-rate cycle early can help with timing shipments or negotiating freight rates.

Exchange Rates

Live foreign exchange rates across major currency pairs.
Why it matters: FX movement directly affects landed cost and margin — worth checking periodically around your payment dates.

World Bank Commodity Price Data

Monthly and annual price data across energy, metals, agriculture, and fertilizer commodities (the "Pink Sheet"), published by the World Bank.
Why it matters: A widely cited, free reference point for commodity price trends — useful supporting data for cost negotiations and forecasting.

Frequently Asked Questions
What does it mean when the PMI drops below 50?

PMI (Purchasing Managers' Index) uses 50 as the dividing line: above 50 signals economic expansion, below 50 signals contraction. A sustained drop below 50 in major economies often precedes a real decline in order volume a few months later, which is why it's used as a leading indicator.

Why should procurement professionals track commodity prices?

Commodity prices are often the underlying basis for supplier quotes. Knowing current price trends lets you judge whether a quote reflects real market conditions and gives you concrete data to reference in negotiations.
